# POSITIONING

This document clarifies how Boundary Protocol should be understood within the broader landscape of system infrastructure and governance technologies.

The goal of this document is to prevent conceptual confusion about the role of the protocol.

Boundary Protocol is **not a control system, not a policy engine, and not a governance framework**. It occupies a distinct role: an **accountability protocol**.

---

# 1. Accountability Protocol

Boundary Protocol is designed to record and structure the accountability trace of autonomous systems.

It answers questions such as:

- What intent was declared?
- What transition was attempted?
- What state was observed?
- Who produced an attestation?
- What verdict was issued?
- Was the verdict disputed?

The protocol therefore provides the infrastructure for **traceable responsibility across system boundaries**.

---

# 2. Not a Control Protocol

Boundary Protocol does not attempt to determine or enforce how systems should behave.

It does not define:

- system permissions
- execution policies
- operational constraints

Systems remain free to operate according to their own rules. The protocol only records the trace of their actions and evaluations.

---

# 3. Not a Governance Framework

Boundary Protocol does not define legal, ethical, or regulatory rules.

Instead, it provides the infrastructure that governance systems may rely on to:

- inspect behavior
- verify claims
- resolve disputes

Governance frameworks may sit **above the protocol**, but they are not part of the protocol itself.

---

# 4. Initial Domain: AI Systems

Autonomous AI systems are the first domain where the need for boundary-level accountability becomes critical.

AI agents increasingly:

- make decisions
- perform actions
- interact with external systems

These systems operate across organizational and technical boundaries, making traditional accountability structures insufficient.

Boundary Protocol provides a structured mechanism to record and evaluate these interactions.

---

# 5. System-Agnostic Infrastructure

Although AI systems are the first target domain, Boundary Protocol is designed to be system-agnostic.

Potential domains include:

- autonomous software systems
- automated infrastructure
- distributed services
- organizational workflows

The protocol defines a **general model of accountable system interaction**.

---

# 6. Relationship to Implementations

Boundary Protocol is a specification.

Implementations may include:

- trace generation SDKs
- verification engines
- dispute resolution systems
- audit tooling

These implementations are separate from the protocol itself and may evolve independently.

---

# 7. Long-Term Vision

The long-term goal of Boundary Protocol is to provide shared infrastructure for recording and verifying the behavior of autonomous systems.

As autonomous systems become more prevalent, the need for reliable accountability traces will grow across many domains.

Boundary Protocol aims to define the minimal common structure required for such accountability to be recorded, verified, and disputed across system boundaries.

---

# Summary

Boundary Protocol should be understood as **infrastructure for accountability**, not infrastructure for control.

It enables systems, organizations, and observers to record and verify how actions occur across boundaries while leaving the control of those systems to their respective environments.
